## PART 1 — THE TOP 10 LEADERS: WHAT THEY ACTUALLY DO

### 1. JEFF BEZOS — Amazon / Blue Origin
**Core Philosophy: Day 1 Thinking + Customer Obsession**

Bezos built the most customer-obsessed company on earth on one principle: it is always Day 1. Day 2 means complacency, then irrelevance, then painful decline.

**What He Says About Success:**
- *"Your brand is what people say about you when you're not in the room."* — Build it by consistently over-delivering.
- *"I knew that if I failed I wouldn't regret that, but I knew the one thing I might regret is not trying."* — Use the Regret Minimization Framework for all major decisions.
- *"We are stubborn on vision, flexible on details."* — Never compromise the north star; iterate ruthlessly on the path.
- *"Invention requires a long-term willingness to be misunderstood."* — Most great ideas look wrong before they look obvious.

**Career Growth Principles:**
- **Work Backwards from the Customer:** Before any project, write the press release first (from the customer's perspective), then the FAQ, then build. This forces clarity of purpose.
- **Write, Don't Slide:** Replace PowerPoint with 6-page narrative memos. Writing forces rigorous thinking. Vague writing reveals vague thinking.
- **Two-Pizza Teams:** Keep execution teams small enough to be fed by two pizzas. Small = fast, accountable, and focused.
- **Disagree and Commit:** Voice dissent clearly during deliberation. Once decided, commit 100%. Endless debate and sullen non-compliance are both failure modes.
- **Hire Bar-Raisers:** Every hire should raise the average of the team. Ask: would I be comfortable if this person's manager saw exactly what I'm doing and why?

**Daily Habits:**
- Protect 8 hours of sleep — non-negotiable.
- No important meetings before 10am; make high-judgment decisions when rested.
- Putter in the morning (read the newspaper, have coffee) — don't rush into reactive mode.
- Eat breakfast with family before work.
- Identify 3 high-quality decisions per day as the goal — not volume of decisions.

### 4. CHARLIE MUNGER — Berkshire Hathaway
**Core Philosophy: Mental Models + Latticework Thinking + Inversion**

Munger's contribution is the most intellectually powerful framework for decision-making: build a latticework of mental models from multiple disciplines, use inversion to find failure modes, and reason from first principles.

**What He Says About Success:**
- *"Invert, always invert. Turn a situation or problem upside down. Look at it backwards."*
- *"It's not supposed to be easy. Anyone who finds it easy is stupid."*
- *"The best thing a human being can do is to help another human being know more."*
- *"I have nothing to add."* (His famous reply when he agrees completely — a model of disciplined restraint.)

**Career Growth Principles:**
- **Build the Latticework:** Learn the foundational models from every major discipline — psychology, physics, biology, economics, mathematics, history. The most creative insights come from cross-domain collision.
- **Inversion:** To achieve X, first ask: what guarantees I fail at X? Systematically avoid all those things.
- **Seek the Pain:** If learning feels comfortable, you're not learning at the edge of your ability. Deliberate discomfort is the signal of genuine growth.
- **The Two-List System:** What do the best people in your field consistently do? Do those things. What do the worst consistently do? Never do those things.
- **Sit with Complexity:** Resist the urge to simplify prematurely. The best decisions come from those willing to hold complexity without collapsing it.

## PART 2 — THE STEP-UP LEADERSHIP FRAMEWORK

The Step-Up Framework defines five levels of leadership behavior, each requiring the full mastery of the level below. Most leaders plateau at level 3. The 1% operate at levels 4 and 5.

### LEVEL 1 — Highly Capable Individual
**What you do:** Produce high-quality individual work. Contribute skills and knowledge effectively.
**Key behaviors:**
- Deliver commitments on time, every time
- Develop rare, valuable technical expertise (T-shaped skills)
- Manage your own energy, focus, and output
- Build reputation through consistent excellence
- Master the Feynman Technique: if you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it

**Transition to Level 2:** Start making others better through your contribution.

---

### LEVEL 2 — Contributing Team Member
**What you do:** Work effectively with others. Contribute beyond your individual role.
**Key behaviors:**
- Make the team's output greater than the sum of parts
- Share knowledge and elevate teammates
- Create psychological safety in your immediate circle
- Solve problems before escalating
- Communicate proactively and transparently

**Transition to Level 3:** Take responsibility for outcomes, not just tasks.

---

### LEVEL 3 — Competent Manager
**What you do:** Organize and motivate people to deliver defined goals.
**Key behaviors:**
- Set clear goals and expectations
- Hold people accountable with care (Radical Candor)
- Delegate outcomes, not tasks
- Run effective 1-on-1s, team meetings, and reviews
- Remove obstacles for your team (servant leadership)
- Develop people through stretch assignments and feedback

**Transition to Level 4:** Define what goals should be set, not just achieve them.

---

### LEVEL 4 — Effective Leader
**What you do:** Set direction and create conditions where teams deliver extraordinary results.
**Key behaviors:**
- Articulate a compelling vision (the Why before the What)
- Create psychological safety at team scale
- Build culture deliberately — it doesn't happen by accident
- Make decisions under uncertainty with confidence
- Develop other leaders (not just performers)
- Think strategically across 1–3 year time horizons
- Build the team: hire bar-raisers, exit gracefully when talent doesn't fit

**Transition to Level 5:** Build organizations that outlast your direct involvement.

---

### LEVEL 5 — Executive / Organizational Builder
**What you do:** Build organizations that achieve extraordinary results systematically.
**Key behaviors:**
- Build culture that sustains performance without your constant presence
- Make strategy and communicate it simply to everyone
- Identify and develop the next generation of Level 4 leaders
- Represent the organization externally with authenticity
- Think in 5–20 year horizons
- Hold the tension between exploitation (today) and exploration (tomorrow)

## PART 3 — THE LEADERSHIP IDENTITY STACK

Based on research from Dweck (Stanford), Kouzes & Posner (The Leadership Challenge), and Sinek:

### Identity Layer 1: Values
Your values are your non-negotiable leadership principles. Write them down explicitly. Examples:
- Truth over comfort
- Excellence over adequacy
- People over process
- Long-term over short-term
- Service over status

### Identity Layer 2: Purpose (Your Why)
Complete this sentence: *"I lead so that _______ can happen, and so that _______ people can experience _______."*

### Identity Layer 3: Leadership Style (Know Your Default)
The research-validated leadership styles (Daniel Goleman, Harvard Business Review):
- **Visionary:** Mobilizes people toward a vision. Most positive impact overall.
- **Coaching:** Develops people for the future. High impact on engagement.
- **Affiliative:** Creates harmony and builds emotional bonds. Useful in conflict.
- **Democratic:** Builds consensus through participation. Useful for buy-in.
- **Pacesetting:** Sets high standards and expects others to follow. High risk of burnout.
- **Commanding:** Demands immediate compliance. Effective only in crisis.

**1% Rule:** Master at least 3 styles and deploy them contextually. Reliance on only one style is a ceiling on your impact.

### Identity Layer 4: Leadership Presence
Based on Sylvia Ann Hewlett's research (Executive Presence):
- **Gravitas (60%):** Confidence, decisiveness, composure under pressure, depth of knowledge, the ability to "read the room." This cannot be faked long-term.
- **Communication (30%):** Clarity, concision, body language calibration, storytelling, the ability to command attention.
- **Appearance (10%):** Polish and professional credibility signals. Least important but first noticed.

## PART 6 — BUILDING PSYCHOLOGICAL SAFETY (Amy Edmondson / Google Project Aristotle)

Google's Project Aristotle studied 180 teams for 5 years to find what made teams perform. The #1 factor, by a significant margin, was **psychological safety** — the belief that you won't be punished for speaking up, taking risks, or making mistakes.

### The Four Stages of Psychological Safety (Timothy Clark)
1. **Inclusion Safety:** I feel safe to be who I am in this group.
2. **Learner Safety:** I feel safe to ask questions, experiment, and make mistakes.
3. **Contributor Safety:** I feel safe to use my skills and add value.
4. **Challenger Safety:** I feel safe to challenge the status quo and offer new ideas.

### How Leaders Build Psychological Safety
- Model vulnerability: share your own uncertainties, mistakes, and growth edges publicly
- When someone speaks up with bad news, thank them first
- Ask questions with genuine curiosity, not interrogation
- Separate the person from the idea — critique ideas, not people
- Respond to failure with "what did we learn?" not "who's responsible?"
- Never punish someone for raising a concern
- Create explicit forums for dissent (pre-mortems, red team reviews)

## PART 7 — DELEGATION MASTERY (Stewardship Delegation)

The #1 leverage multiplier for leaders is effective delegation. Most leaders delegate tasks (what to do). The 1% delegate outcomes (what success looks like).

### The Stewardship Delegation Framework (Covey)
When delegating, communicate clearly on five dimensions:
1. **Desired Results:** What does success look like? What outcome, by when?
2. **Guidelines:** The constraints. What must not be violated? (Values, legal, ethical)
3. **Resources:** What people, money, tools, and information are available?
4. **Accountability:** How will we measure progress? When and how will we check in?
5. **Consequences:** What happens if it succeeds? What happens if it fails?

**The most common delegation failure:** Giving the task but not the authority to execute it. If you delegate the outcome, you must also delegate the decision-making power to achieve it.

### The 4 Levels of Delegation
| Level | What You Say | When to Use |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Level 1 | "Here's what to do. Do exactly this." | Crisis, new person, non-negotiable compliance required |
| Level 2 | "Here's the situation. Come back with options." | Developing person, want their thinking |
| Level 3 | "Here's the goal. Decide and act, but keep me informed." | Trusted person, defined domain |
| Level 4 | "Here's the domain. You own it. I trust your judgment." | Senior team members with full domain ownership |

## PART 8 — TALENT DEVELOPMENT

The best leaders obsessively develop the people around them. Jack Welch: *"Before you are a leader, success is all about growing yourself. When you become a leader, success is all about growing others."*

### The Three Conversations Every Leader Must Have
1. **The Performance Conversation:** How are you doing against the goals? What do you need from me?
2. **The Development Conversation:** What do you want to get better at? What's the one thing that, if you mastered it, would change your career trajectory?
3. **The Career Conversation:** Where do you want to go? How does what you're doing today serve that goal? What do I need to know about your ambitions?

### Stretch Assignment Design
The most powerful development tool is a stretch assignment: a real project that is 15–30% beyond the person's current capability.
- **Too easy:** No growth, declining engagement
- **Just right (15–30% stretch):** Maximum learning, high engagement, builds capability
- **Too hard (>50% beyond current skill):** Overwhelm, failure, confidence damage

### Feedback Architecture
The most effective feedback is:
- **Specific:** Not "good job" but "the way you handled the pushback from the client by acknowledging their concern before reframing the solution was exactly right"
- **Immediate:** Within 24 hours of the behavior
- **Behavioral:** About what the person did, not who they are
- **Forward-looking:** "Next time, try..."
- **Balanced:** The Gottman ratio applies — 5 positive to 1 corrective

## PART 10 — THE 90-DAY LEADERSHIP FAST-START

### Days 1–10: Foundation
- Complete the self-assessment above. Identify your 3 gaps.
- Write your Leadership Philosophy: 1 page, what you believe about leading people.
- Have a "getting to know you" conversation with every direct report. Ask: what's working, what's not, and what do they need from you?
- Identify your team's single biggest bottleneck. Commit to removing it.

### Days 11–30: Communication & Feedback
- Implement weekly 1-on-1s for all direct reports (30 min minimum)
- Give specific, behavioral feedback to every team member at least once this month
- Start using the Pyramid Principle in all written communication (conclusion first)
- Run one pre-mortem on your biggest current initiative
- Have one difficult conversation you have been avoiding

### Days 31–60: Execution & Systems
- Build OKRs for your team (1 Objective, 3-4 Key Results)
- Create a delegation map: what do only you do? What can others own?
- Implement a weekly team review ritual: wins, blockers, priorities
- Run your first After-Action Review on a completed project
- Identify and develop your highest-potential team member with a stretch assignment

### Days 61–90: Culture & Development
- Write explicit team norms: how we make decisions, how we communicate, how we handle conflict
- Have a career conversation with each team member
- Build your personal board of advisors (3–5 leaders further along than you)
- Start mentoring someone junior
- Document 3 lessons learned from this 90-day period and share them with your team
